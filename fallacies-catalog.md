## Formal Fallacies

These are errors in the **logical form** of an argument — the conclusion doesn't follow from
the premises regardless of whether the premises are true.

### Affirming the Consequent
- **Form:** If P then Q. Q. Therefore P.
- **Example:** "If it rains, the ground is wet. The ground is wet, so it must have rained."
- **Diagnostic:** Could Q be true for another reason?

### Denying the Antecedent
- **Form:** If P then Q. Not-P. Therefore not-Q.
- **Example:** "If you study, you'll pass. You didn't study, so you won't pass."
- **Diagnostic:** Could Q follow from something other than P?

### Undistributed Middle
- **Form:** All A are B. All C are B. Therefore all A are C.
- **Example:** "All dogs are mammals. All cats are mammals. So all dogs are cats."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the shared predicate actually connect the subjects?

### Illicit Major / Illicit Minor
- Occurs in syllogisms when a term in the conclusion is more general than its use in the premises.

### Fallacy of Four Terms (Quaternio Terminorum)
- A syllogism with four terms due to equivocation on one word.

### Existential Fallacy
- Drawing an existential conclusion from purely universal premises.

### Masked Man Fallacy (Intensional Context Error)
- Treating intensional (belief-relative) contexts as extensional.
- **Example:** "I know who the president is. The president is a spy. Therefore I know a spy."

## Informal — Relevance

Premises that are **irrelevant** to the conclusion, though they may feel persuasive.

### Ad Hominem (Abusive)
- Attacking the **person** rather than their argument.
- **Example:** "You can't trust her analysis — she's been divorced twice."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the personal attack actually undermine the argument's logic?
- **Note:** Ad hominem is valid when *credibility* is the direct subject of the claim.

### Ad Hominem (Circumstantial)
- Dismissing an argument because the arguer would benefit from it being true.
- **Example:** "Of course the oil company says climate policy will hurt jobs."
- **Note:** Self-interest is *relevant evidence of bias* but not a refutation of the argument.

### Tu Quoque (Appeal to Hypocrisy)
- "You do it too, so your criticism is invalid."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the other party's behavior affect the truth of the claim?

### Straw Man
- Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.
- **Diagnostic:** Would the original arguer recognize your characterization as fair?

### Red Herring
- Introducing an irrelevant issue to distract from the main argument.

### Appeal to Emotion (Argumentum ad Passiones)
- Using emotional language/imagery as a *substitute* for reasoning.
- **Note:** Emotion is *relevant* when the subject matter concerns emotional stakes.

### Appeal to Force (Ad Baculum)
- Threats or coercion used in place of evidence or reasoning.

### Appeal to Pity (Ad Misericordiam)
- Exploiting sympathy rather than giving reasons.
- **Note:** Pity IS relevant when the question is about welfare or fairness.

### Appeal to Flattery (Ad Captandum)
- Winning agreement through compliment rather than argument.

### Genetic Fallacy
- Judging an argument's merit by its **origin** rather than its content.
- **Example:** "That idea came from a known fraud, so it must be wrong."
- **Note:** Origin can be *relevant evidence* for probability of truth, but not determinative.

### Appeal to Motive / Bulverism
- Explaining away an argument by psychologizing the arguer's motives.

## Informal — Presumption

Arguments that **assume what they need to prove** or smuggle in unwarranted assumptions.

### Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)
- The conclusion is assumed in the premises (circular reasoning).
- **Example:** "The Bible is true because the Bible says it is."
- **Diagnostic:** Would someone who doubts the conclusion also doubt the premise?

### False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)
- Presenting only two options when more exist.
- **Example:** "You're either with us or against us."
- **Diagnostic:** Are there other genuine alternatives being ignored?

### Loaded Question (Complex Question)
- Presupposes a controversial claim in the question itself.
- **Example:** "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?"
- **Note:** This is a **rhetorical** fallacy, not always an argument failure.

### Slippery Slope
- Claiming a first step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without justification.
- **CONSERVATIVE FLAG:** Only flag if *no causal mechanism* is given. When empirical evidence
  of causal chains exists, slippery slope reasoning is **valid**.
- **Example (fallacious):** "If we allow gay marriage, next people will marry animals."
- **Example (valid):** "Studies show that small initial debt often leads to larger default
  (cite mechanism)."

### Hasty Generalization
- Drawing a broad conclusion from an insufficient or unrepresentative sample.
- **Diagnostic:** How large and representative is the sample? Could selection bias explain it?

### Ecological Fallacy
- Applying group-level statistics to individual cases.

### No True Scotsman
- Redefining a category to exclude counterexamples.
- **Example:** "No true Christian would do that. — But he's a Christian. — Then he's not a
  *true* Christian."

### Naturalistic Fallacy (Is-Ought)
- Deriving a normative conclusion solely from a factual premise.
- **Example:** "Animals kill each other in nature, therefore killing is morally permissible."

### Appeal to Nature
- Arguing something is good/bad simply because it is/isn't natural.

### Appeal to Tradition (Ad Antiquitatem)
- "We've always done it this way, so it must be right."

### Appeal to Novelty (Ad Novitatem)
- "It's new, so it must be better."

### Nirvana Fallacy (Perfect Solution Fallacy)
- Rejecting a solution because it isn't perfect.

### Middle Ground Fallacy (False Balance)
- Assuming the truth lies between two positions simply because two positions exist.

## Informal — Ambiguity

Arguments that exploit **unclear or shifting meanings**.

### Equivocation
- Using a word with multiple meanings interchangeably as if they're the same.
- **Example:** "The sign said 'fine for parking here.' So I parked — it's fine."

### Amphiboly
- Ambiguity arising from grammatical structure rather than individual words.

### Accent Fallacy
- Changing meaning by placing emphasis on different words.

### Composition Fallacy
- What is true of parts is assumed true of the whole.
- **Example:** "Each player on this team is excellent, so the team must be excellent."

### Division Fallacy
- What is true of the whole is assumed true of each part.

### Reification (Hypostatization)
- Treating an abstract concept as a concrete thing.

### Motte and Bailey
- Defending an extreme position (bailey) by retreating to a modest one (motte) when
  challenged, then re-advancing the extreme position.
- **Diagnostic:** Does the arguer switch between versions of their claim?

## Informal — Weak Induction

Arguments where evidence **exists but is insufficient** to support the conclusion.

### Appeal to Ignorance (Ad Ignorantiam)
- "It hasn't been disproved, so it must be true" (or vice versa).
- **CONSERVATIVE FLAG:** In scientific contexts, absence of evidence IS evidence of absence
  when good testing has been done. Only flag when the lack of evidence is simply due to
  no one having looked.

### Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum / Bandwagon)
- "Millions of people believe X, so X must be true."
- **Note:** Consensus *among experts* is relevant evidence; popular consensus on technical
  matters is much weaker.

### Appeal to Authority (Ad Verecundiam)
- **CONSERVATIVE FLAG:** Only flag when: (a) the authority is not relevant to the domain,
  (b) the field lacks genuine expert consensus, or (c) the citation is used to *shut down*
  rather than *inform* debate.
- Valid: citing a virologist on viral transmission
- Fallacious: citing a physicist on nutritional science

### Appeal to Anecdote
- Treating a personal story as representative data.
- **Diagnostic:** Is the anecdote being used to prove a general claim, or to illustrate a
  claim proven elsewhere?

### False Analogy
- Basing an argument on a comparison where the relevant similarities don't hold.
- **CONSERVATIVE FLAG:** Analogies that are genuinely structurally parallel are **valid
  reasoning**. Only flag when the disanalogies are *relevant* to the conclusion.

### Weak Analogy (Faulty Analogy)
- The analogy has some validity but the differences outweigh the similarities for purposes
  of the argument.

## Informal — Causal

Errors in reasoning about **cause and effect**.

### Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
- "A preceded B, therefore A caused B."
- **Example:** "I wore my lucky socks and we won — the socks caused the win."
- **Diagnostic:** Is there a plausible mechanism? Could both be caused by a third factor?

### Cum Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
- Correlation implies causation.
- **Diagnostic:** Is a confounding variable likely? Has directionality been established?

### Reverse Causation
- Assuming the direction of causality without justification.

### Overdetermination / Misattribution
- Attributing an effect to a cause when multiple causes are present.

### Gambler's Fallacy
- Believing past random events affect future independent ones.
- **Example:** "The coin has come up heads 5 times — tails is 'due.'"

### Hot-Hand Fallacy
- Believing streaks in random processes will continue.
- **Note:** In some domains (sports, finance) streaks *may* be non-random — use caution.

## Informal — Statistical

### Base Rate Neglect
- Ignoring prior probability when evaluating evidence.

### Survivorship Bias
- Drawing conclusions only from the visible successes, ignoring failures.

### Selection Bias
- The sample systematically differs from the population in a relevant way.

### Texas Sharpshooter
- Identifying a pattern in data *after* seeing it, then acting as if it was predicted.

### Moving the Goalposts
- Changing the standard of proof after initial evidence is provided.

### Cherry Picking (Suppressed Evidence)
- Selecting only favorable data while ignoring contradicting data.

## Diagnostic Cheat Sheet

| Symptom | Suspect |
|---------|---------|
| Attacks the person, not the argument | Ad Hominem |
| Two choices when more exist | False Dilemma |
| "Everyone knows / agrees" | Ad Populum |
| "Scientists say / experts agree" (vague) | Appeal to Authority |
| Conclusion assumed in premise | Begging the Question |
| Original position changes under scrutiny | Motte & Bailey / Moving the Goalposts |
| Events in sequence → causation assumed | Post Hoc |
| Correlation presented as causation | Cum Hoc |
| Part → Whole or Whole → Part | Composition / Division |
| Word shifts meaning mid-argument | Equivocation |
| Modest step → catastrophic end (no mechanism) | Slippery Slope |
| Personal example used as general proof | Appeal to Anecdote |
| Misrepresents opponent's view | Straw Man |
| "If not proven false, must be true" | Appeal to Ignorance |
| Group data applied to individual | Ecological Fallacy |
| Standard raised after first evidence | Moving the Goalposts |
| Only successes cited | Survivorship Bias / Cherry Picking |
